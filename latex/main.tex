\begin{abstract}
This paper presents CircuitCraft, an open-source modification (``mod'') for the sandbox
game Minecraft that transforms the game world into an analog electronics laboratory. Players
place resistor, capacitor, inductor, memristor, diode, and ideal op-amp blocks, wire them
together using conductive blocks, drive them with DC and time-varying sources, and probe the
resulting voltages and currents with a pair of handheld oscilloscope probes -- one
time-domain, one X-Y -- all rendered as ordinary game objects placed and manipulated with the
same mechanics used to construct anything else within the game. In contrast to scripted or
table-driven approximations of circuit behavior, every electrical quantity in CircuitCraft
is produced by a genuine modified nodal analysis (MNA) solver of the kind
employed in SPICE-family simulators, including a charge-controlled linear-drift memristor
model, a linearized diode, and an ideal op-amp modeled as a nullor. A separate, complex-valued
small-signal (AC) solver, sharing the same wiring topology, additionally lets a player sweep a
dedicated AC source across a configurable frequency range and read the result as a Bode plot --
including a two-pole frequency-dependent gain model for the op-amp, since an ideal, infinite-bandwidth
nullor could never itself exhibit the rolloff and phase shift such a plot exists to show. This
paper describes
which of Minecraft's own construction
mechanics the mod builds upon, the system's software architecture, the circuit-theoretic
design decisions underlying it (in particular, an explicit, player-placeable ground
reference), the pedagogical rationale for embedding a correct solver within a game
environment rather than a dedicated simulator, and the verification methodology employed
during development. The paper concludes with a discussion of current limitations and a
roadmap for classroom evaluation.
\end{abstract}
